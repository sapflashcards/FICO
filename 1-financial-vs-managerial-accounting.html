üè¶ PART 1: Financial Accounting (FI)

üí° What is Financial Accounting?

Simple explanation: Think of it as your company's official money diary. Every dollar that comes in or goes out gets written down here. This is what you show to the government, investors, and banks.

The General Ledger System

üè™ Accounts Payable (AP)

üìí General Ledger (G/L)

üîë Key Point: When you record something in a sub-ledger (AR, AP, or Assets), it automatically updates the General Ledger at the same time. You don't have to enter it twice!

Real-Life Examples

üìò Accounts Receivable Example (AR)

Scenario: A customer buys $1,000 worth of bikes but will pay in 30 days.

What happens in SAP:

  • Customer's account shows: "You owe us $1,000"
  • General Ledger automatically records: "Total receivables increased by $1,000"
  • Balance Sheet shows: Assets went up by $1,000 (we have a right to receive money)

When customer pays: Their account clears, and receivables in G/L decrease by $1,000

üìï Accounts Payable Example (AP)

Scenario: We receive a $500 invoice from a bike parts supplier.

What happens in SAP:

  • Supplier's account shows: "We owe them $500"
  • General Ledger automatically records: "Total liabilities increased by $500"
  • Balance Sheet shows: Our debts went up by $500

üìó Asset Accounting Example

Scenario: Company buys a new factory machine for $50,000.

What happens in SAP:

  • Asset record created for the machine with full lifecycle tracking
  • General Ledger records: "Fixed assets increased by $50,000"
  • Over time, depreciation automatically reduces the asset value (machines wear out!)

Understanding Cost Types

1Ô∏è‚É£ Primary Costs

Where from: Financial Accounting (FI)

What: Real money spent on external things

Examples:

  • Buying raw materials ($10,000)
  • Employee salaries ($5,000)
  • Electricity bills ($500)

2Ô∏è‚É£ Secondary Costs

Where from: Internal transfers within company

What: When one department "charges" another

Examples:

  • IT department provides service to Sales dept
  • Maintenance fixes Production machinery
  • No actual money leaves the company!

3Ô∏è‚É£ Revenue Elements

Where from: Financial Accounting (FI)

What: Money earned from sales

Examples:

  • Sold 100 bikes for $50,000
  • Service fees: $2,000
  • This is what we compare costs against

Detailed Area Breakdown

üè≠ Product Cost Controlling

Purpose: Track costs for specific products or orders

Two main parts:

  • Product Cost Planning: "We estimate making 10 bikes will cost $500" (planning ahead)
  • Cost Object Controlling: "Making those 10 bikes actually cost $520" (tracking actual costs)

Example: A customer orders 10 custom bikes. SAP creates a "sales order" as the cost object and tracks every dollar spent making those bikes‚Äîmaterials, labor, machine time, etc.

üè¢ Overhead Cost Controlling

Purpose: Handle costs that can't be directly tied to a product

What are overheads? Costs that keep the business running but aren't directly in the product.

Examples:

  • Marketing campaign costs $10,000‚Äîhelped sell all products, but which one specifically?
  • HR department salaries
  • Rent for office building

Solution: These costs are collected in "cost centers" (departments) then distributed to products using allocation methods.

üìà Margin Analysis

Purpose: Answer the question: "Are we actually making money?"

What it does: Combines ALL costs + revenues to show profitability

Real Example Question It Answers:

"What profit did we make selling bike parts to Reseller A in North America last quarter?"

  • Revenue from Reseller A: $100,000
  • Direct product costs: $60,000
  • Overhead allocated: $20,000
  • Operating Profit: $20,000 ‚úÖ

‚öñÔ∏è Side-by-Side Comparison

üè¶ Financial Accounting (FI)

  • Purpose: Legal reporting to outsiders
  • Who sees it: Government, investors, banks, auditors
  • Time focus: Historical (what already happened)
  • Level of detail: Company-wide totals
  • Flexibility: Must follow strict accounting rules
  • Example question: "What was our total profit last year?"

üéØ Management Accounting (CO)

  • Purpose: Internal decision-making
  • Who sees it: Managers, executives (internal only)
  • Time focus: Future planning and control
  • Level of detail: By department, product, project
  • Flexibility: Whatever helps the business
  • Example question: "Which product line is most profitable?"
üîó How They Work Together:

Financial Accounting feeds data into Management Accounting. When you spend money (recorded in FI), it gets categorized and analyzed (in CO) to help managers understand WHERE and WHY money was spent, and whether it was worth it!

üéØ Key Takeaways

Remember These Simple Concepts:

  • General Ledger = The master book where everything is recorded
  • Sub-ledgers (AR, AP, Assets) = Detailed tracking that automatically updates the General Ledger
  • FI to CO flow = Actual transactions (FI) get analyzed and categorized (CO) for decision-making
  • Primary costs = Real money spent outside the company
  • Secondary costs = Internal charges between departments
  • Product Cost Controlling = Tracking costs for specific products/orders
  • Overhead Controlling = Managing indirect costs (marketing, admin, etc.)
  • Margin Analysis = The final answer: Did we make money?

üö¥ Complete Bike Company Example

Scenario: Customer orders 10 custom bikes for $5,000

  1. FI: Records sale of $5,000 in General Ledger (revenue) and Accounts Receivable (customer owes us)
  2. CO - Cost Elements: Categorizes costs: $2,000 materials, $1,500 labor, $500 machine use
  3. CO - Product Cost: Tracks all costs against this specific 10-bike order
  4. CO - Overhead: Adds $300 for marketing and admin costs
  5. CO - Margin Analysis: Revenue $5,000 - Total costs $4,300 = Profit $700 ‚úÖ

Result: Management knows this order was profitable and can make more informed decisions!
